## 1. The Tone Framework

### From Personality to Copy

The brand's `personality_traits` in the config are the compass for all writing. Here's
how to translate them into actual sentences:

| If the trait is... | The writing should... | Example |
|--------------------|-----------------------|---------|
| **Confident** | Use declarative statements, avoid hedging | "We deliver" not "We try to deliver" |
| **Approachable** | Use "you" and "we", conversational contractions | "You'll love this" not "One will appreciate" |
| **Professional** | Avoid slang, maintain subject expertise | "Our analysis shows" not "We kinda think" |
| **Playful** | Use wordplay, light humor, unexpected twists | "Goodbye, boring spreadsheets" |
| **Innovative** | Emphasize newness, possibility, forward-thinking | "The future of X starts here" |
| **Trustworthy** | Back claims with evidence, be transparent | "Based on 10,000 customer reviews" |
| **Bold** | Use short punchy sentences, strong verbs | "Build faster. Ship sooner. Win more." |
| **Warm** | Show empathy, use inclusive language | "We're with you every step of the way" |

### The Voice Spectrum

Every brand sits somewhere on these spectrums. The config's personality traits determine
the position:

```
Formal ←————————————→ Casual
Technical ←————————————→ Simple
Serious ←————————————→ Playful
Reserved ←————————————→ Enthusiastic
Abstract ←————————————→ Concrete
```

When writing any piece of copy, calibrate against these spectrums. A "professional yet
approachable" brand sits at roughly 40% from the formal end and 30% from the serious end.

### The "Read Aloud" Test

Every piece of copy should pass this test: if a brand spokesperson read it aloud at a
conference, would it feel natural and on-brand? If it sounds robotic, too stiff, or too
casual for the brand, revise.

## 2. Platform-Specific Voice Adaptation

The brand voice stays consistent, but its expression adapts to each platform's context.
Think of it like a person who speaks differently at a board meeting vs. a coffee chat —
same personality, different register.

### LinkedIn

**Register**: Professional thought leadership
**Adaptation rules**:
- Open with a hook or provocative statement (first 2 lines are the "fold")
- Use data points and industry insights to build credibility
- 3-5 short paragraphs, each making one point
- End with a question or call-to-discussion
- Emoji: 0-2, used structurally (bullet-point style) not decoratively
- Hashtags: 3-5, industry-relevant

**Structure template**:
```
[Hook — surprising stat, bold claim, or question]

[Context — why this matters now]

[Insight — your brand's unique perspective]

[CTA — question or invitation to engage]

#RelevantHashtag #BrandHashtag
```

### Instagram

**Register**: Visual storytelling, lifestyle-oriented
**Adaptation rules**:
- Caption supports the image — don't repeat what's visible
- Open with the most compelling line (first line shows in feed)
- Short paragraphs with line breaks for readability
- Emoji: 2-5, contextual (not random)
- Hashtags: 5-15, mix of broad and niche, in a separate paragraph or first comment
- CTA: "Link in bio", "Save this", "Share with someone who..."

**Structure template**:
```
[Opening hook — ties to the visual]

[Value or story — 2-3 short sentences]

[CTA — engagement-focused]

.
.
.
#Hashtag1 #Hashtag2 #BrandHashtag
```

### Twitter / X

**Register**: Punchy, conversational, real-time
**Adaptation rules**:
- One core idea per tweet
- Front-load the value (no preamble)
- Use line breaks for emphasis
- Threads for longer stories (max 5-7 tweets)
- Emoji: 0-2
- Hashtags: 1-2 max (integrated into text naturally)

### Facebook

**Register**: Community-oriented, storytelling
**Adaptation rules**:
- More conversational than LinkedIn, more substantial than Twitter
- Questions drive engagement
- Stories and customer spotlights work well
- 300-500 characters optimal for engagement
- Links are fine (Facebook penalizes less than other platforms)

### WeChat Official Account (微信公众号)

**Register**: Informative, relationship-focused
**Adaptation rules**:
- Longer-form content is expected and welcomed
- Title is critical — it determines open rates
- Use headers, images, and formatting to break up text
- QR codes for engagement (subscribe, contact, purchase)
- More formal than Western social media
- Consider both Simplified and Traditional Chinese audience needs

### Xiaohongshu (小红书)

**Register**: Authentic, personal, lifestyle-oriented
**Adaptation rules**:
- First-person perspective works best ("I discovered...", "My experience with...")
- Heavy emoji usage is expected and natural on this platform
- Structure as tips, lists, or mini-guides
- 10-20 hashtags (mix of trending and brand-specific)
- Images/videos are the primary content; text supports them
- Authentic tone crucial — overly commercial content performs poorly

### Email Marketing

**Register**: Personal, value-forward
**Adaptation rules**:
- Subject line: 30-50 characters, benefit-first, create urgency or curiosity
- Preview text: Complement (don't repeat) the subject line
- Body: One primary CTA, scannable sections, mobile-friendly
- Tone: As if writing to one person, not a list
- Sign-off: Use a real person's name when possible

## 3. Copy Templates by Deliverable Type

### Poster / Banner Copy

**Hero headline**: 3-7 words. Active verb. Emotional or benefit-driven.
**Subheadline**: 10-15 words. Expands on the headline with specifics.
**Body (if any)**: 20-40 words maximum. Key details only.
**CTA**: 2-4 words. Action verb + object.

```
Example:
Headline: "Design Without Limits"
Subheadline: "The all-in-one platform that grows with your creative vision"
CTA: "Start Free Today"
```

### Presentation Copy

**Title slide**: Brand name + presentation topic + date/audience
**Section headers**: 3-6 words, active and descriptive
**Slide body**: Max 6 bullet points, each under 15 words
**Supporting data**: Label clearly, source at bottom

The "10-20-30 rule" (Guy Kawasaki): 10 slides, 20 minutes, 30pt minimum font.
Adapt to your brand's needs but keep slides lean.

### Brochure / Company Introduction

**Cover**: Brand name, tagline, hero image
**About section**: Mission + founding story in 50-100 words
**Value proposition**: 3-5 key benefits with supporting details
**Proof points**: Customer stats, awards, certifications
**CTA page**: Clear next step + contact information

### Video Script / Storyboard

**Hook** (0-3 seconds): Attention-grabbing visual or statement
**Problem** (3-10 seconds): Establish the pain point
**Solution** (10-25 seconds): Introduce the brand's answer
**Proof** (25-40 seconds): Evidence, demo, testimonial
**CTA** (40-45 seconds): What to do next

Adapt length to platform: 15s for social ads, 30-60s for brand stories, 2-3min for explainers.

### Press Release

```
[Headline — factual, newsworthy, under 80 characters]
[Subheadline — additional context]
[City, Date] — [Opening paragraph: who, what, when, where, why in 2-3 sentences]
[Quote from executive — brand voice in first person]
[Body — 2-3 paragraphs of details]
[Boilerplate — standard company description from brand config]
[Contact information]
```

## 4. Writing Quality Checklist

Before finalizing ANY copy, run through this checklist:

**Brand Voice**
- Does the copy reflect the brand's personality traits?
- Would it pass the "read aloud" test?
- Is the tone appropriate for the platform?

**Clarity**
- Can someone understand the main message in 5 seconds?
- Are there any jargon or insider terms that need simplification?
- Is the CTA clear and actionable?

**Consistency**
- Are preferred terms used (check terminology in brand config)?
- Is capitalization consistent with brand rules?
- Is the date/number format consistent?

**Engagement**
- Is there a hook in the first line?
- Does the copy create value for the reader (inform, inspire, entertain)?
- Is there a clear reason to take the next step?

**Correctness**
- Grammar and spelling checked?
- All claims verifiable?
- Legal disclaimers included where required?
- Trademarks properly attributed?

## 5. Common Pitfalls

### Pitfall 1: "Corporate Speak"
Brands that value professionalism often drift into lifeless corporate language. Watch for:
buzzwords (synergy, leverage, paradigm), passive voice overuse, noun stacks
("customer engagement optimization strategy").

Fix: Replace with plain language. "We help teams work better together" beats "We provide
integrated collaboration solutions."

### Pitfall 2: Inconsistent Personality
Copy that's playful in social media but robotic on the website creates brand confusion.
The voice should feel like the same person across all channels — just adjusting their
register for the setting.

### Pitfall 3: Feature Dumping
Listing features without connecting to benefits. Every feature should answer "so what?"
for the reader.

"Our platform has 256-bit encryption" → "Your data is protected by bank-grade security"

### Pitfall 4: Cultural Blind Spots
When writing for international audiences, watch for idioms that don't translate, cultural
references that don't resonate, and humor that might not land. When in doubt, simplify
and make it more universal.

### Pitfall 5: Ignoring Platform Norms
A 500-word LinkedIn post crammed into a tweet. A casual Instagram style on a formal website.
Always adapt the voice to the platform without losing the brand personality.
